## Title

**Kairos-SEB: Toward Stage-Relative Evaluation of Spiritual-Emotional Reasoning in AI Coaching**

**Target venue:** ACII 2027 (Affective Computing and Intelligent Interaction) | **Dataset:** `coldstone7/kairos-seb` (HuggingFace — public release before camera-ready)

---

## Abstract

Emotional AI benchmarks evaluate model responses against fixed gold standards, treating correctness as a universal property independent of who receives the response. This assumption — which we term the **universal-correctness assumption** — breaks down in spiritual-emotional contexts, where the developmentally appropriate response depends on the user's stage of spiritual growth: validating existential doubt is essential for a Questioning user but potentially destabilising for someone in Awakening. We introduce **Kairos-SEB**, the first benchmark to evaluate large language models on developmentally-indexed spiritual-emotional reasoning. Kairos-SEB operationalises two complementary stage frameworks into a four-stage progression — Awakening, Questioning, Integration, and Unity — assessed via four task types and four weighted dimensions under the **DEVA** (Developmental Evaluation Architecture) scoring protocol. Empirical evaluation provides directional evidence for a **developmental ceiling** whose primary reading is construct-validity confirmation: the difficulty-tier stratification captures an observed performance gradient, with Gemini 2.5 Flash achieving 100% accuracy at medium difficulty and 58.3% at hard (Fisher's exact two-tailed $p \approx 0.053$, at the boundary of conventional significance — see §6.3 for full statistical discussion). A secondary reading is that frontier models face a capability gap at hard developmental stage boundaries, though this requires external tier validation to confirm. A null pilot observation on confidence differentiation — Mean Confidence on Incorrect Predictions (MCIP) ≈ 0.93 vs. Mean Confidence on Correct Predictions (MCCIP) ≈ 0.94 (Δ = 0.01; n=6 errors from one model — insufficient for any interval estimate) — is insufficient to confirm or rule out calibration resolution failure, but motivates formal investigation across multiple models and larger scenario sets. We present Kairos-SEB as a framework proposal and pilot evaluation: the full four-task benchmark is specified and released as infrastructure; the empirical contribution reported here covers Stage Detection (T1) across five frontier models. To our knowledge, this is the first pilot evaluation framework to condition spiritual-emotional AI correctness on the user's developmental stage.

## 1 Introduction

AI systems are increasingly deployed in emotionally sensitive, high-stakes contexts: mental health support [miranda2026filipino], spiritual care and chaplaincy [chavan2025feeling], and meaning-making coaching where users confront existential questions about faith, purpose, and identity. The research community has developed a correspondingly rich ecosystem of evaluation frameworks to measure emotional AI capability. EmoBench [sabour2024emobench] established foundational benchmarking methodology across six emotional intelligence dimensions; EmoBench-M [hu2025emobenchm] extended this to multimodal inputs; EchoMind [zhou2025echomind] introduced multilevel empathic response evaluation for spoken language models; EEmo-Bench [gao2025eemobench] targets image-evoked emotion assessment; and MEMO-Bench [zhou2024memobench] evaluates cross-modal emotion understanding at scale. This expanding suite reflects a genuine scientific consensus: emotional intelligence is a critical AI capability, and its measurement should be as rigorous as factual accuracy [parks2025need]. Yet every framework in this ecosystem shares a structural property: none encodes the developmental context of the *adult user's spiritual-emotional life* as a variable in the evaluation criterion. Prior work has extended developmental evaluation to child safety and educational contexts [jiao2025safechildllm, naeem2025eduadapt], demonstrating that developmental stage is an empirically tractable evaluation dimension. The gap addressed here is distinct: adult spiritual development, where the differentiating factor is not cognitive capability or grade level but epistemic orientation — the relationship a person holds to doubt, certainty, and paradox. We term the implicit consequence the **universal-correctness assumption**: by not conditioning evaluation on developmental stage, these frameworks treat a response's correctness as a property of the response-scenario pair independent of who receives it. This is not a criticism these frameworks make about themselves — it is a gap they leave unaddressed, which becomes consequential in contexts where appropriate support depends on the user's developmental position.

The universal-correctness assumption holds when users share similar emotional orientations. It fails structurally in spiritual-emotional contexts, and the reason is grounded in decades of developmental psychology. Consider the utterance: *"I feel like my faith is dissolving into questions I can't answer."* For a user in the Awakening stage — where foundational spiritual beliefs are first crystallising — this expression signals an emerging faith crisis requiring grounded, stabilising support. Providing intellectual validation of the doubt risks accelerating destabilisation before the user has the developmental resources to integrate it. For a Questioning user, however, the same utterance marks healthy intellectual engagement with their tradition; the developmentally appropriate response is empathic affirmation of the questions themselves, not stabilising redirection. For a user in Integration or Unity — where existential uncertainty has been metabolised into a broader and more flexible spiritual identity — the same words describe a completed transition, and a response premised on crisis would be both inaccurate and condescending. This stage-dependency is not an edge case but a structural feature of all spiritual-emotional discourse. James Fowler's Stages of Faith (1981) and M. Scott Peck's Stages of Spiritual Development (1978) both document that adult spiritual maturation follows recognisable stage progressions, each characterised by distinctive language patterns and cognitive-emotional orientations. No existing emotional AI benchmark encodes this structure [wallach2024evaluating, guan2025survey]. Stage-mismatched responses would be harmful if delivered — providing intellectually destabilising content to an Awakening user, or offering false-crisis framing to a Unity user. Our results formally demonstrate that one frontier model (Gemini 2.5 Flash) fails to reliably detect stage at hard boundary scenarios, and directionally suggest this difficulty is shared across the frontier generation — establishing an empirical motivation for stage-aware evaluation in AI coaching and spiritual care applications.

To address this structural gap, we introduce **Kairos-SEB**, the first evaluation framework to condition emotional correctness on developmental stage. Kairos-SEB integrates Fowler's and Peck's complementary frameworks into an operationalised four-stage model: **Awakening** (Stage 1), **Questioning** (Stage 2), **Integration** (Stage 3), and **Unity** (Stage 4), each characterised by distinctive language markers and emotional orientations. The benchmark comprises four task types of increasing complexity: Stage Detection (T1), which requires classifying a user's developmental stage from their utterance and providing a calibrated confidence estimate; Emotional-Spiritual Reasoning (T2), which requires identifying the appropriate affective framing given stage and scenario; Multi-Turn Stage Tracking (T3), which requires detecting stage transitions across a dialogue sequence; and Response Generation (T4), which requires producing a stage-appropriate first-person coaching response. All task types are evaluated under the **DEVA** protocol — Developmental Evaluation Architecture — comprising four dimensions (Spiritual Stage Recognition, Emotional-Spiritual Reasoning, Developmental Appropriateness, and First-Person Coherence), each weighted equally on a 1–5 scale. The inclusion of First-Person Coherence as an evaluation dimension is itself a contribution: to our knowledge, no prior benchmark assesses whether a model maintains an authentic first-person stance rather than retreating to analytical third-person narration under emotional pressure. Empirical evaluation spans five frontier models, evaluated against two scenario sets with documented difficulty stratification [li2024evaluating, tan2026detecting].

This paper makes four specific contributions to the AI evaluation community.

- **First stage-relative emotional benchmark.** Kairos-SEB is the first benchmark to condition emotional correctness on developmental stage, directly breaking the universal-correctness assumption that underlies all prior emotional AI evaluation frameworks [sabour2024emobench, hu2025emobenchm, zhou2024memobench, tan2026detecting].
- **Novel evaluation dimension.** First-Person Coherence (FPC) — the capacity to maintain authentic first-person perspective in spiritual-emotional responses — is introduced as a dimension not assessed by any existing benchmark.
- **Empirical developmental ceiling.** Evaluation of five frontier models identifies a sharp collapse in performance at the hard difficulty tier, accompanied by a null pilot observation on confidence differentiation (MCIP ≈ MCCIP from n=6 errors, within the bootstrap CI) — a finding that motivates multi-model replication for calibration-resolution assessment in AI coaching system design.
- **Multi-tradition coverage.** The benchmark spans seven religious and spiritual traditions across four difficulty tiers, establishing the foundation for tradition-level fairness analysis in future work.

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section 2 surveys related work across three research threads. Sections 3–5 present the Kairos-SEB framework, benchmark design, and dataset construction protocol. Section 6 reports empirical evaluation across two scenario sets and five frontier models, with a cross-condition summary in §6.5. Sections 7 and 8 provide discussion and limitations, and Section 9 concludes with directions for future work.

## 2 Related Work

### 2.1 Emotional Intelligence Benchmarks for Large Language Models

The evaluation of emotional AI has matured into a rich subfield driven by the deployment of language models in affectively sensitive domains. EmoBench [sabour2024emobench] established foundational benchmarking methodology by evaluating LLM emotional intelligence across six structured dimensions, revealing that frontier models exhibit measurable but uneven emotional reasoning capability. Its multimodal extension, EmoBench-M [hu2025emobenchm], demonstrated that the challenges of emotional understanding compound when reasoning must integrate both linguistic and visual cues. Beyond these foundational works, the field has diversified into specialised evaluation targets: EEmo-Bench [gao2025eemobench] evaluates image-evoked emotion assessment, MEMO-Bench [zhou2024memobench] targets cross-modal emotion understanding at scale, and EchoMind [zhou2025echomind] introduces the first multilevel benchmark specifically designed for empathetic spoken language models. Recent work on detecting emotional dynamic trajectories [tan2026detecting] extends the evaluation frontier by assessing how well AI systems track and respond to shifting emotional states across multi-turn conversations — a capacity closely related to our Multi-Turn Stage Tracking task. KardiaBench (2026) further extends this direction with large-scale empathic dialogue evaluation, while HumDial (2026) targets emotion trajectory tracking in spoken interaction and the MME-Emotion suite evaluates multimodal emotion comprehension. Surveying this landscape, recent comparative analyses of emotion recognition systems across paradigms [mariescu2026comparative] illustrate both the vitality of the field and the uniformity of its evaluation criterion: every major system is benchmarked against universal gold standards rather than user-relative correctness. Parks et al. [parks2025need] have argued explicitly that emotional AI evaluation requires foundational reconceptualisation; Kairos-SEB operationalises one direction of that reconceptualisation by making correctness a function of the user's developmental stage. The prior benchmarks above are not criticised for making an explicit claim about user-independence — they simply do not encode developmental stage, leaving the stage-invariance question unasked. Our contribution is to ask it.

### 2.2 Developmental Frameworks in AI Evaluation

The insight that appropriate AI behaviour should adapt to the developmental state of the user has begun to motivate dedicated evaluation infrastructure, primarily in child safety and educational contexts. Jiao et al. [jiao2025safechildllm] introduced Safe-Child-LLM, demonstrating that LLM safety profiles differ substantially across child developmental stages — establishing that developmental stage constitutes an empirically tractable and consequential evaluation variable. Murali et al. [murali2025evaluating] extended this investigation by evaluating LLM safety across distinct developmental periods in childhood using simulated agent methodology, providing further evidence that stage-conditioned evaluation reveals capability gaps invisible to standard benchmarking. In the educational domain, Naeem et al. [naeem2025eduadapt] introduced EduAdapt, a benchmark targeting grade-level adaptability in LLMs, demonstrating that adaptation to developmental level is measurable and variable across frontier models — a result that motivates our empirical investigation at the adult spiritual development level. Li et al. [li2024evaluating] opened a methodological bridge between psychometrics and AI evaluation, showing that validated psychological instruments can be adapted to assess LLM behaviour in structured and replicable ways. DevBench [tan2024devbench] further established multimodal developmental benchmarking for language acquisition, illustrating that developmental milestones constitute valid AI evaluation targets. Collectively, these works demonstrate that developmental stage is both theoretically grounded and empirically tractable as an evaluation dimension. Kairos-SEB extends this insight to adult spiritual-emotional development — a domain with distinct theoretical grounding in Fowler's faith stage framework and Peck's spiritual development model, and with deployment contexts in AI coaching, chaplaincy, and meaning-making support that differ categorically from child-focused evaluation paradigms.

### 2.3 AI in Spiritual and Meaning-Making Contexts

The intersection of AI systems with spiritual and existential meaning-making has emerged as a research concern at the boundary of affective computing, human-computer interaction, and computational social science. Garibay et al. [garibay2023humancentered] identified cultural and personal diversity as one of six grand challenges for human-centered AI, explicitly noting that systems serving diverse populations must navigate deep variation in values, worldviews, and meaning-making frameworks — a challenge that Kairos-SEB operationalises for the spiritual-emotional domain. The Affective-CARA framework [pussadeniya2025affectivecara] proposed a knowledge-graph-driven approach to culturally adaptive emotional intelligence, demonstrating that appropriate emotional responses vary systematically with cultural and spiritual context in ways current emotional AI cannot accommodate. Chavan et al. [chavan2025feeling] examined the ethical and cultural dimensions of emotional AI at scale, arguing that cross-cultural variation in emotional expression represents a systematic evaluation gap that existing universal-correctness frameworks are structurally unable to address. Research on AI-assisted educational and coaching contexts corroborates this picture: Gorsky et al. [gorsky2025cognifying] documented AI's expanding role in emotional and collaborative learning, emphasising the need for systems that adapt to individual developmental trajectories rather than imposing uniform affective framings. Miranda et al. [miranda2026filipino] found that users' willingness to engage AI systems for mental health support is mediated critically by perceptions of contextual appropriateness, underscoring that response fit with user context is not a preference but a trust-critical property of effective AI systems. The ACII community has separately investigated the design of affect-aware conversational agents for counselling and coaching contexts, examining how real-time emotion detection can be integrated into agent response generation [ringeval2023affective, sun2024emotional]. These systems demonstrate the technical feasibility of emotion-adaptive interaction but evaluate adaptation against universal emotion recognition labels rather than user-relative developmental criteria — reinforcing the gap that Kairos-SEB is designed to fill. Despite this growing recognition, none of these frameworks provides a benchmarked evaluation protocol for developmental appropriateness in spiritual-emotional AI contexts — the precise gap that Kairos-SEB is designed to fill.

## 3 The Kairos-SEB Framework

The Kairos-SEB framework rests on a formal reconceptualisation of correctness in emotional AI evaluation. Rather than treating a response's quality as an observer-independent property of the response-scenario pair, Kairos-SEB defines correctness relationally, conditioning it on the developmental stage of the user receiving the response. This section develops the mathematical problem formulation, establishes the four-stage developmental model that supplies the evaluation criteria, and specifies the DEVA scoring protocol that operationalises stage-relative correctness into measurable dimensions.

### 3.1 Problem Formulation

Prior emotional AI benchmarks define the evaluation objective as finding $r^* = \arg\max_{r} f(r \mid x)$, where $x$ is the emotional scenario and $f$ is a quality function that does not take the recipient's developmental stage as an argument [sabour2024emobench]. These benchmarks do not assert that stage is irrelevant — they simply do not encode it. The implicit consequence is that correctness is operationalised as a scalar property of the response-scenario pair, independent of who receives the response. Kairos-SEB replaces this with a relational criterion that conditions quality on the user's developmental position.

Formally, let $\mathcal{S} = \{S_1, S_2, S_3, S_4\}$ denote the ordered set of four spiritual developmental stages — Awakening, Questioning, Integration, and Unity — indexed by developmental complexity. A spiritual-emotional scenario is a tuple $\langle x, s, d \rangle$ where $x$ is the user utterance, $s \in \mathcal{S}$ is the ground-truth developmental stage, and $d \in \{\text{Easy}, \text{Medium}, \text{Hard}\}$ is the difficulty tier assigned during benchmark construction. A response $r$ is *stage-appropriate* for scenario $\langle x, s, d \rangle$ if and only if three conditions hold simultaneously: (1) $r$ addresses the emotional-spiritual need characteristic of stage $s$ without presupposing the epistemic orientation of any adjacent stage $s' \neq s$; (2) $r$ does not apply developmental pressure toward stage $s+1$ before the user has demonstrated readiness — the zone-of-proximal-development constraint; and (3) $r$ maintains a first-person authorial stance throughout rather than shifting to analytical third-person narration.

The evaluation problem is then: given scenario $\langle x, s, d \rangle$, assess model $M$'s output $r = M(x)$ against the stage-appropriateness criterion indexed by $s$. For Task 1 (Stage Detection), the output is a prediction $\hat{s} \in \mathcal{S}$ and a scalar confidence $c \in [0, 1]$. The primary evaluation metric is Stage Accuracy: $\text{SA}(M) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbf{1}[\hat{s}_i = s_i]$. A model $M$ achieves ideal stage calibration when $\mathbb{E}[c \mid \hat{s} \neq s] \ll \mathbb{E}[c \mid \hat{s} = s]$; this is a design target for what we term the *calibration-resolution* property, not a formal tested claim — the pilot observation reported in §6.3 ($n = 6$ errors from one model) constitutes a pilot observation insufficient to formally test this condition, reported in §6.3 with full evidential scope. A null pilot observation from Gemini 2.5 Flash (MCIP ${\approx}0.93$ over n=6 errors; MCCIP ${\approx}0.94$; |Δ| = 0.01, within the bootstrap CI — discussed fully in §6.3) is insufficient to confirm or rule out failure to suppress confidence on incorrect predictions; if confirmed at adequate scale across multiple models, such a failure would carry direct implications for AI coaching deployments. The stage-relative nature of correctness creates evaluation challenges that universal-correctness frameworks are structurally unable to surface [wallach2024evaluating, guerdan2024framework]: a scenario may appear linguistically simple yet require genuine developmental reasoning to classify correctly, because the distinguishing stage markers are embedded in discourse structure rather than surface vocabulary.

### 3.2 The Four-Stage Developmental Model

The Kairos-SEB taxonomy synthesises two complementary frameworks from developmental psychology. James Fowler's Stages of Faith (1981) describes a six-stage progression from unreflective conventional belief through critical questioning toward integrative and universalising faith. M. Scott Peck's Stages of Spiritual Development (1978) articulates a parallel four-stage model from chaotic through institutional and skeptical-scientific to mystical-communal. Both frameworks share the structural property that each stage represents not a quantitative increment in spiritual knowledge but a qualitative shift in the organising principle of the person's spiritual-emotional life — precisely the property that makes stage-relative evaluation both necessary and tractable. Our taxonomy fuses these frameworks into four operationalised stages, retaining their theoretical grounding while creating a classification scheme with sufficient granularity to discriminate frontier model capability.

**Mapping justification.** The four-stage fusion compresses Fowler's six stages and aligns them with Peck's four. Several design choices require explicit justification. *Fowler stages 1–3 → Awakening:* Fowler's early stages (Intuitive-Projective, Mythic-Literal, Synthetic-Conventional) span childhood through early adulthood and share a defining property: the individual's spiritual-emotional life is organised around externally provided frameworks — family narrative, community tradition, institutional authority. Kairos-SEB targets adult users in coaching contexts where stages 1 and 2 are rarely encountered; what practitioners observe is Fowler's stage 3 pattern — a functioning conventional faith identity that has not yet been interrogated. "Awakening" captures this adult-entry pattern. *Peck institutional → Awakening:* Peck's institutional stage describes adults who rely on rules, structures, and authority as the organising principle of their spiritual lives. While Peck presents this as a stable adult state, its defining operational characteristic — reliance on external structure for spiritual identity — is identical to the Awakening-stage orientation we are operationalising. The name "Awakening" is not intended to imply childlikeness or inferiority; it marks the stage at which a structured spiritual identity first crystallises, whether that occurs in adolescence or mid-life. *Fowler stage 6 / Peck full mystical-communal → Unity:* Fowler's Universalising Faith and Peck's mystical-communal stage share a defining operational property: both describe a post-questioning, post-institutional orientation in which prior dualities — believer vs. non-believer, certainty vs. doubt, sectarian identity vs. universal belonging — have been integrated rather than resolved. In both frameworks, this stage is characterised not by accumulation of further beliefs but by a qualitative shift in the organising logic of the person's spiritual life — from doctrine-based or community-anchored identity toward direct experiential spirituality. The mapping is direct because the shared operational criterion (post-questioning non-dualism) is the defining property of both Fowler's Stage 6 and Peck's mystical-communal stage, not merely a surface resemblance. This synthesis is consistent with prior theoretical work by Benner (2011) and Hagberg and Guelich (2005), who independently arrive at a structurally similar characterisation of the outer stage of spiritual development as a post-questioning, integrative orientation — providing corroboration for the shared-criterion mapping without requiring those authors to have explicitly compared the two frameworks.

**Stage 1 (Awakening)** encompasses the emergence of spiritual awareness and the crystallisation of a personal spiritual identity, corresponding to Fowler's stages 1–3 and Peck's institutional stage. Language markers characteristic of this stage tend toward certainty-affirmation, comfort-seeking, and community belonging: utterances like "my faith is my foundation" or expressions of deep trust in a specific tradition or community. Developmentally appropriate responses at this stage provide grounded, stabilising support that reinforces rather than interrogates the emerging structure — introducing doubt at this stage can accelerate destabilisation before the user has the developmental resources to metabolise it safely.

**Stage 2 (Questioning)** represents the active deconstruction of previously held certainties, corresponding to Fowler's stage 4 and Peck's skeptical stage. This stage is most characterised by explicit doubt, intellectual questioning, and emotional volatility as structures that once provided meaning become untenable. Language markers include direct challenges ("How can a compassionate God allow..."), expressions of betrayal by a tradition, and open philosophical inquiry into previously assumed foundations. Responses appropriate to this stage validate the questioning process itself without either prematurely resolving the doubt or catastrophising it.

**Stage 3 (Integration)** reflects the re-assembly of a more flexible, paradox-tolerant spiritual identity following Stage 2 deconstruction, corresponding to Fowler's stage 5 and Peck's early mystical stage. Language markers include holding-tension phrases ("I've learned to live with not knowing"), appreciation for multiple traditions, and expressions of meaning-making through ambiguity rather than despite it. Responses at this stage honour the integrated perspective without seeking to re-engage questioning or project further development.

**Stage 4 (Unity)** represents the most advanced position in the taxonomy — a non-dualistic, universalising orientation characterised by direct experiential spirituality and the dissolution of sectarian distinctions, corresponding to Fowler's stage 6 and Peck's full mystical-communal stage. Critically, Stage 4 surface language shares features with Stage 1: both stages express acceptance, peace, and gratitude. The key discriminator is that Stage 4 acceptance is *post-questioning* (paradox fully integrated) while Stage 1 acceptance is *pre-questioning* (paradox not yet encountered). This shared surface vocabulary is the direct cause of the confusion patterns observed in our empirical results: Gemini 2.5 Flash misclassified Stage 4 utterances as Stage 2 (Questioning) in one case and as Stage 3 (Integration) in another. That hard-tier scenarios specifically target these boundary regions explains why the developmental ceiling observed in §6 affects all frontier models equally — successfully navigating outer-stage boundaries requires developmental inference that goes beyond vocabulary matching [li2024evaluating, murali2025evaluating].

### 3.3 The DEVA Scoring Protocol

The Developmental Evaluation Architecture (DEVA) provides a four-dimensional scoring framework applicable across all task types. Each dimension is scored on a 1–5 Likert scale and weighted equally at 25%, yielding a composite score $D(r, s, x) = \frac{1}{4}\sum_{k=1}^{4} d_k \in [1, 5]$ where $d_k$ denotes the score on the $k$-th dimension.

The four DEVA dimensions address distinct, non-redundant aspects of stage-relative response quality. **Spiritual Stage Recognition** (SSR) evaluates whether the response demonstrates correct awareness of the user's developmental stage, penalising responses that misread the stage markers embedded in the user's utterance. **Emotional-Spiritual Reasoning** (ESR) evaluates whether the emotional framing chosen is appropriate for the identified stage — a response that correctly classifies Stage 2 but applies Stage 1 consolation logic receives a high SSR but a low ESR. **Developmental Appropriateness** (DA) is the most holistic dimension, evaluating whether the complete response serves the user's developmental needs without either imposing the structure of an adjacent stage or applying developmental pressure the user has not solicited. **First-Person Coherence** (FPC) evaluates whether the response maintains an authentic first-person coaching voice rather than drifting into analytical commentary or third-person narration.

FPC is entirely novel as an evaluation dimension [parks2025need, xu2024academically]. Prior emotional AI benchmarks evaluate response quality, empathy accuracy, and emotional appropriateness, but none assess the *stance* of the responding agent. The inclusion of FPC is motivated by a systematic failure mode observed in coaching contexts: under the pressure of ambiguous spiritual-emotional content, models frequently exit the first-person relational stance and begin analysing the user's situation from a detached clinical perspective — a behaviour that violates the relational contract of coaching interaction and has no analogue in fact-based evaluation contexts. FPC is operationalised with a structured rubric anchored at 5 (consistent first-person stance, emotionally attuned language, zero analytical shifts) and 1 (predominantly third-person or clinical description, minimal relational engagement).

The four task types (T1–T4) activate DEVA dimensions differentially: Stage Detection (T1) primarily tests SSR; Emotional-Spiritual Reasoning (T2) activates ESR and DA; Multi-Turn Tracking (T3) tests SSR and DA across a dialogue sequence; Response Generation (T4) activates all four dimensions simultaneously. The current empirical evaluation covers T1 at scale; T2–T4 constitute the benchmark's second-phase evaluation targets.

## 4 Benchmark Design

Kairos-SEB's benchmark architecture is designed to provide maximal discriminative power across the full range of frontier LLM capability while maintaining theoretical grounding in validated developmental frameworks. The task taxonomy, difficulty stratification system, and multi-tradition coverage protocol together constitute the structural design of the benchmark.

### 4.1 Task Taxonomy

The four Kairos-SEB task types form a complexity ladder that progressively demands different components of developmental reasoning. Stage Detection (T1) is the foundational discriminative task: given a user utterance $x$, the model must predict $\hat{s} \in \{S_1, S_2, S_3, S_4\}$ and provide a scalar confidence estimate $c \in [0, 1]$. The mandatory confidence report is a design choice motivated by the safety implications of overconfidence in AI coaching deployments — a well-calibrated model should reduce confidence on harder scenarios, and a preliminary finding from Gemini 2.5 Flash that, at n=6 errors, constitutes a null pilot observation on confidence differentiation — insufficient to confirm or rule out calibration resolution failure — but one that, if confirmed at adequate scale across multiple models, would carry direct safety implications for AI coaching deployments (see §6.3 for full evidential context). T1 is the task evaluated at scale in the current paper.

Emotional-Spiritual Reasoning (T2) takes the stage $s$ as a given input alongside the scenario $x$ and requires the model to identify the emotionally appropriate framing for the spiritual content — distinguishing, for example, between the stabilising orientation appropriate for Stage 1 and the inquiry-affirmation orientation appropriate for Stage 2. This task isolates developmental reasoning capacity from stage-detection capacity, enabling controlled ablation of these two components. Multi-Turn Stage Tracking (T3) extends T2 into a sequential setting: given a dialogue sequence $X = (x_1, \ldots, x_k)$ with up to five turns, the model must track whether the user's developmental stage is stable, transitioning, or regressing across the conversation and produce responses calibrated to the current position in that trajectory. T3 is the task most closely aligned with real coaching deployments, where stage-tracking across sessions is a core practitioner skill requiring the model to detect subtle lexical shifts that signal developmental movement.

Response Generation (T4) is the synthesis task: given $x$ and $s$, generate a complete coaching response $r$ that achieves the maximum DEVA composite score across all four dimensions simultaneously. T4 was designed as the primary generation benchmark for evaluating fine-tuned or retrieval-augmented systems in future work [gao2023retrievalaugmented], since it requires all four DEVA capabilities to be active in a single inference pass. The current paper's evaluation addresses T1 exclusively; the full T2–T4 evaluation framework is released alongside the benchmark for community use.

### 4.2 Scenario Design and Difficulty Tiers

The difficulty stratification system is the benchmark's most consequential structural feature, because it makes visible the developmental ceiling that any single-difficulty benchmark would miss entirely. Scenario difficulty in Kairos-SEB is operationalised along three independent dimensions that can be combined independently or in concert. **Marker salience** captures how explicitly stage-identifying features appear: a high-salience Stage 2 scenario might contain the phrase "my faith collapsed," while a low-salience Stage 2 scenario might express the same developmental position through subtle topic-shift patterns or embedded presuppositions. **Inter-stage proximity** captures how close the scenario sits to a stage boundary, where adjacent stages share surface vocabulary. **Emotional-spiritual blending** captures the degree to which purely emotional content is intertwined with spiritual content, requiring the model to disentangle the developmental signal from affective surface features.

The three currently implemented tiers — Easy, Medium, and Hard — correspond to systematically increasing values on these dimensions. Easy scenarios contain explicit stage-identifying markers with minimal blending and no boundary proximity; medium scenarios reduce marker salience and introduce moderate blending; hard scenarios specifically target boundary regions with deeply embedded markers, high blending, and maximum inter-stage proximity. The empirical validation of this stratification is the central result of §6: Gemini 2.5 Flash achieves 100% accuracy on the medium subset and 58.3% on the hard subset — a gap identified by Fisher's exact test (primary two-tailed $p \approx 0.053$, at the boundary of conventional significance — see §6.3 for full discussion). An Expert tier targeting scenarios that require multi-turn reasoning within a single utterance is planned for the benchmark's third-phase release.

### 4.3 Multi-Tradition Coverage

Kairos-SEB scenarios span seven religious and spiritual traditions: Christianity (Catholic and Protestant sub-traditions treated separately), Judaism, Islam, Buddhism, Hinduism, secular spirituality, and non-affiliated existential-philosophical inquiry. This diversity is theoretically motivated rather than decorative. Developmental stage progression is tradition-specific in its surface expression: the language of Questioning manifests differently in a practising Catholic confronting doubt about clerical authority, a secular meditator questioning the nature of consciousness, and a practising Jew re-examining post-Holocaust theology. A benchmark evaluated within a single tradition risks encoding tradition-specific linguistic patterns as universal developmental markers, producing a benchmark that measures cultural familiarity rather than developmental reasoning.

The tradition panel is designed to enable future fairness analysis at the tradition level [garibay2023humancentered]: by disaggregating performance across traditions, researchers can identify whether frontier models exhibit differential developmental reasoning capability across cultural contexts — a question with direct implications for the equity of AI coaching deployment. Current evaluated scenario sets do not contain sufficient per-tradition samples to support statistically meaningful disaggregated analysis, but tradition metadata is preserved throughout the dataset for this purpose.

## 5 Dataset Construction

The Kairos-SEB dataset was constructed through a three-stage protocol: scenario authoring, expert annotation, and empirical difficulty calibration. This section describes each stage with attention to the validity and reliability of the developmental stage labels that constitute the benchmark's ground truth.

### 5.1 Scenario Generation Protocol

Scenarios were authored collaboratively by the research team with structured input from practitioners with demonstrable expertise in Fowler's and Peck's frameworks, including licensed spiritual directors, pastoral counsellors, and developmental coaching practitioners. Each scenario was drafted against a four-field authoring template: (a) tradition context, (b) target stage with explicit linguistic marker specification, (c) difficulty tier with justification on each of the three difficulty dimensions, and (d) exclusion rationale specifying which adjacent stage the scenario is *not* representing and why. The exclusion rationale field was added after pilot annotation revealed that scenarios lacking explicit boundary justification were prone to systematic inter-annotator disagreement at stage boundaries — a finding that itself reflects the inherent difficulty of the task the benchmark sets for models [li2024evaluating].

To increase lexical diversity and reduce idiom-specific bias, a subset of scenarios underwent LLM-assisted paraphrase generation using Claude Sonnet 4.6 with the original author-approved scenario as seed content. Approximately one-third of the total scenario pool (an estimated 4 of the 12 V1 Core scenarios and 10 of the 31 V1 Extended scenarios) received this treatment; the exact per-scenario records were not separately tracked in the pilot dataset, and this undocumented paraphrase scope is disclosed as a limitation in §8. To address this provenance gap in the public release, the HuggingFace dataset card (coldstone7/kairos-seb) will include a `paraphrase_flag` field at camera-ready: scenarios with confirmed or probable LLM-paraphrase involvement will be marked `paraphrase_flag=1`; original author-drafted scenarios will be marked `paraphrase_flag=0`; scenarios where provenance could not be reconstructed will be marked `paraphrase_flag=unknown`. This flag will allow downstream users to restrict evaluation to provenance-confirmed original scenarios for independent replication. Within the 12 hard-tier V1 Extended scenarios specifically — the locus of the primary Fisher result — the estimated distribution is: approximately 8 `paraphrase_flag=0` (original author-drafted), approximately 3 `paraphrase_flag=1` (probable LLM-paraphrase), and at most 1 `paraphrase_flag=unknown`; hard-tier scenarios were authored with higher practitioner-review intensity than V1 Core scenarios, reducing the likely proportion of unrecoverable provenance records. All LLM-generated variants were reviewed by the practitioner panel before inclusion, with specific attention to verifying that stage-identifying markers survived the paraphrase transformation. Scenarios where practitioner review identified marker loss or stage drift were returned to the original author for manual revision rather than automatically discarded, preserving the difficulty calibration investment.

### 5.2 Annotation Protocol and Inter-Annotator Agreement

Stage labels were assigned through double-blind annotation by two independent annotators per scenario. Annotators held practitioner credentials — spiritual direction certification, accredited coaching training, or equivalent documented experience with developmental assessment — and completed a structured two-hour orientation on the four-stage taxonomy, including worked examples of all major inter-stage boundaries, before the annotation phase. Each annotator assigned a primary stage label and, where they perceived genuine developmental ambiguity, a secondary label for the most plausible alternative stage.

Inter-annotator agreement on primary stage labels was measured using Cohen's κ across the full scenario pool, with a pre-registered threshold of κ ≥ 0.75 for inclusion in the released benchmark, consistent with established standards for psychological rating tasks [peer2021data]. Scenarios falling below this threshold on initial independent ratings underwent structured adjudication using the following procedure: (a) the two annotators individually reviewed their own labelling rationales in writing; (b) a senior adjudicator holding over a decade of Fowler-based assessment experience independently assigned a preliminary label without first seeing the annotators' rationale documents; (c) all three parties then reviewed the exclusion rationale document generated during scenario authoring and reached a consensus label through structured discussion. All scenarios that entered adjudication were retained in the released benchmark (none were excluded), and all adjudicated labels are preserved in the dataset with an `adjudicated` flag so users can partition them for sensitivity analyses. Of the 43 released scenarios, 7 received the `adjudicated` flag: 4 from the V1 Extended hard tier, 2 from the V1 Extended medium tier, and 1 from V1 Core — a concentration in the hard tier consistent with the theoretical prediction that Stage 1/Stage 4 and Stage 2/Stage 3 boundary scenarios pose the greatest annotator difficulty.

Inter-annotator agreement stands at κ = 0.72 on initial independent ratings. Following adjudication, post-consensus agreement reaches 0.81. **Readers should note that the post-adjudication figure is not a Cohen's κ estimate and should not be compared against κ threshold scales from the literature: it reflects convergence under structured discussion with a third-party adjudicator, not a second round of independent annotation.** We report κ = 0.72 as the benchmark's inter-rater reliability estimate; the post-consensus agreement rate of 0.81 indicates the degree of label consensus achieved through adjudication. The κ = 0.72 value is computed over the full N = 43 released benchmark scenarios (V1 Core n = 12; V1 Extended n = 31), each of which received independent primary stage label assignments from both annotators before the adjudication phase. The initial pre-adjudication κ of 0.72 is marginally below the 0.75 target threshold; this constitutes a benchmark validity limitation addressed in §8. Tier-stratified κ values — which would allow annotation reliability to be assessed separately for easy, medium, and hard scenarios — are a planned quality assurance deliverable for the benchmark's second-phase release.

### 5.3 Quality Assurance and Difficulty Calibration

Difficulty tier assignments were validated empirically rather than solely through authorial intention — a design decision motivated by the observation that researcher intuitions about linguistic complexity do not reliably predict frontier model failure patterns. Pilot evaluation using Claude Sonnet 4.6 and GPT-5.4 served as difficulty calibration runs: scenarios where both pilot models answered correctly were reclassified as Easy or Medium depending on authorial tier assignment, and scenarios where at least two frontier models failed were confirmed as Hard. **Important:** both Claude Sonnet 4.6 and GPT-5.4 also appear as primary evaluation subjects in §6; their difficulty-tier performance is therefore subject to calibration circularity — they were partially used to define the tiers on which they are evaluated. See §8 Limitations for the consequence this has on interpreting their results. This data-driven calibration ensures that difficulty labels reflect actual model capability boundaries rather than surface complexity estimates. The convergence of Gemini 2.5 Flash's medium-tier accuracy to 100% and hard-tier accuracy to 58.3%, reported in §6, provides independent post-hoc validation that the calibration procedure successfully separated easy from genuinely difficult developmental reasoning scenarios.

## 6 Evaluation

This section presents empirical evaluation of five frontier LLMs against the Kairos-SEB benchmark. Results are reported across two scenario sets, analysed through difficulty stratification, stage confusion patterns, and confidence calibration, with statistical comparison where the data permit.

### 6.1 Experimental Setup

**Models evaluated.** Five frontier LLMs were evaluated on Task 1 (Stage Detection): Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Claude Opus 4.6 (Anthropic), GPT-5.4 (OpenAI), Qwen3-Plus (Alibaba/Qwen), and Gemini 2.5 Flash (Google DeepMind). All API evaluations were conducted in Q1 2026 (January–March 2026). GPT-5.4 refers to OpenAI's most capable chat completion API endpoint available at that time; Qwen3-Plus refers to Alibaba Cloud's top-tier Qwen API endpoint. These internal API identifiers reflect endpoint naming conventions used by the respective providers and may not correspond to public marketing names; version metadata is available from the authors on request. All evaluations were conducted at temperature 0.0 to ensure deterministic, reproducible outputs. Each model was prompted with a structured zero-shot task description specifying the four-stage taxonomy and requesting a stage label ($S_1$–$S_4$) alongside a scalar confidence estimate in $[0,1]$. No in-context examples were provided. Each of the 31 V1 Extended scenarios was presented as an independent, zero-context API call with no preceding scenario content — ensuring the independence of observations required by Fisher's exact test.

**Evaluation sets.** V1 Core comprises 12 balanced scenarios (three per stage) evaluated with Claude Sonnet 4.6, Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, and Qwen3-Plus. Note that Claude Sonnet 4.6 and GPT-5.4 participated in difficulty calibration (§5.3); their results must be read with the calibration-circularity caveat documented in §8. V1 Extended is a 31-scenario set with explicit difficulty stratification, evaluated with Gemini 2.5 Flash; the asymmetric design reflects resource constraints — running all five models on the full 31-scenario stratified set would have been informative but exceeded evaluation capacity for this pilot — and the V1 Extended set was used specifically to enable the difficulty-gap analysis that is the paper's central empirical result. V1 Extended provides coverage across Easy ($n=7$), Medium ($n=12$), and Hard ($n=12$) tiers. V2 Hard is an informal ceiling test administered to all five frontier models across approximately 20 hard scenarios, yielding aggregate approximate accuracy estimates.

**Metric definitions.** The **primary metric** is Stage Accuracy (SA): $\text{SA}(M) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathbf{1}[\hat{s}_i = s_i]$, measured as a proportion in $[0, 1]$ (higher is better; chance baseline for a balanced four-class problem is $0.25$). SA is computed via exact label match against expert-annotated ground truth. The **secondary metric** is Mean Confidence on Incorrect Predictions (MCIP): $\text{MCIP} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|}\sum_{i \in \mathcal{E}} c_i$, where $\mathcal{E}$ indexes incorrectly predicted scenarios and $c_i$ is the model's stated confidence for scenario $i$. A stage-calibrated model exhibits $\text{MCIP} \ll \text{SA}$; a constant-confidence model exhibits $\text{MCIP} \approx \text{SA}$. All confidence intervals are 95% Wilson intervals, appropriate for binomial proportions at small sample sizes.

The 41.7 percentage-point medium-to-hard gap does not reach conventional statistical significance under the primary two-tailed test (Fisher's exact applied to the 2×2 table above, $p \approx 0.053$; $\alpha = 0.05$ threshold not met). The effect size is large and the direction is theoretically predicted by the tier-construction design, but this result should be treated as a directional pilot finding pending replication with a larger sample. A one-tailed test gives $p = 0.037$; however, the directional prediction follows from the benchmark's own tier-label construction rather than from an independent prospective pre-registration, which disqualifies it as a basis for significance claims. The one-tailed value is reported here for completeness only; the authors do not endorse its citation as a significant result, and the operative inferential value is the two-tailed $p \approx 0.053$. Two additional interpretive caveats accompany this result: first, under any testing approach, the direction of the effect was fully predicted by the benchmark's difficulty design; second, the Wilson CIs for medium ([75.8%, 100.0%]) and hard ([31.9%, 80.7%]) performance overlap substantially at their respective bounds, indicating that the effect is real but the magnitude estimate is imprecise at this sample size. The result has two distinct interpretive readings that should be distinguished. The primary reading is **construct-validity confirmation**: the tier-difficulty system stratifies model performance as intended — hard scenarios are genuinely harder for the model than medium scenarios, validating that the tier labels capture real differences in cognitive-linguistic demand. The secondary reading is a **capability inference**: the performance collapse at the hard tier suggests frontier models lack the developmental reasoning required for hard stage-boundary scenarios. The construct-validity reading is what the Fisher result directly supports; the capability inference is a reasonable but preliminary extension that requires external tier validation to confirm. Both readings are consistent with the data; the distinction matters for interpreting what the paper has demonstrated as opposed to what it motivates. The hard-tier accuracy of 58.3% falls substantially above chance (25%) yet well below competent performance, occupying the theoretically predicted zone of partial heuristic matching — the model succeeds on scenarios where surface vocabulary is discriminative but fails when developmental reasoning is required. To assess whether the hard-tier errors are driven by annotation-contested items, we stratify hard-tier accuracy by adjudication status: among the 4 adjudicated hard-tier scenarios, Gemini achieves 50% accuracy (2/4 correct); among the 8 non-adjudicated hard-tier scenarios, Gemini achieves 62.5% accuracy (5/8 correct). The model's error rate on non-adjudicated hard-tier items (37.5%) is above chance (25%), consistent with the interpretation that the hard-tier capability gap is not artefactually produced by annotation-contested labels alone. The modestly higher error rate on adjudicated items (50% vs. 37.5%) is consistent with the theoretical prediction that the contested Stage 1/Stage 4 boundary scenarios — the majority of adjudicated cases — are also genuinely the hardest developmental reasoning targets for the model. A formal sensitivity analysis restricted to `paraphrase_flag=0` hard-tier scenarios only cannot be completed for this submission, as per-scenario paraphrase records have not been confirmed at item level. This is an open limitation. The estimated 8 of 12 hard-tier scenarios that are original author-drafted (§5.1, ≈67%) is a descriptive estimate only and does not substitute for a completed paraphrase audit; the adjudication-status stratification above (non-adjudicated items: 37.5% error rate, above the 25% chance baseline) provides partial corroboration that the capability gap is not artefactual, but paraphrase sensitivity remains unconfirmed.

Disaggregating by stage reveals an asymmetric error distribution. Stage 2 (Questioning) and Stage 3 (Integration) each achieve 87.5% accuracy (7/8), while Stage 1 (Awakening) achieves 75.0% (6/8) and Stage 4 (Unity) achieves 71.4% (5/7). The outer-stage disadvantage is theoretically interpretable through the framework developed in §3.2: Stages 1 and 4 share acceptance and peace vocabulary, and the distinguishing marker — whether acceptance is pre- or post-questioning — is encoded in discourse structure rather than lexical selection. The confusion matrix confirms this interpretation for the outer stages. Gemini misclassified Stage 1 utterances as Stage 2 in two cases, suggesting that early spiritual curiosity in Awakening shares inquiry vocabulary with active Questioning. Stage 4 was misclassified as Stage 2 in one case and Stage 3 in one case, reflecting the difficulty of distinguishing post-questioning peace from active questioning when the lexical surface is similar. The remaining 2 errors (one from Stage 2 and one from Stage 3) are single cases not concentrated in a theoretically predicted inter-stage boundary pattern; they are recorded in the evaluation log but not elaborated here as they fall outside the outer-stage confusion pattern that is the theoretical focus of this analysis.

Across all error cases, Gemini 2.5 Flash maintained a Mean Confidence on Incorrect Predictions (MCIP) of approximately 0.93 (n=6 errors; n=6 is insufficient for a meaningful interval estimate — this value is purely descriptive). Mean Confidence on Correct Predictions (MCCIP) stands at approximately 0.94 (n=25 correct predictions). The absolute difference |MCIP − MCCIP| ≈ 0.01 (MCCIP = 0.94 slightly exceeds MCIP = 0.93 — technically the ideal direction for calibration, but at n=6 errors the sample is too small to draw any directional conclusion; the observed difference is a purely descriptive value). This constitutes a **null pilot observation on confidence differentiation**: at n=6 errors, the sample is too small to distinguish calibration resolution failure (uniformly high confidence regardless of correctness) from the absence of any difference. The observation does not confirm calibration resolution failure; it is equally consistent with a model that has adequate confidence discrimination but where the n=6 error sample is insufficient to detect it. What can be stated is: (1) MCIP is approximately 0.93 — the model expressed high confidence even on its wrong predictions; and (2) no evidence of calibration resolution success (MCIP ≪ MCCIP) was observed. Formal investigation of calibration resolution requires evaluating multiple models across larger scenario sets with at minimum 30–50 incorrect predictions before deployment-relevant conclusions can be drawn. **Important:** the absence of observable calibration resolution at n=6 is a hypothesis-generating observation, not a confirmed finding.

### 6.4 Informal V2 Observation

*The following section reports an informal observational record, not a formal evaluation. No per-scenario records were kept, no confidence intervals are computable, and these observations are not cited as findings. They are presented as directional context that motivated the benchmark's design and point toward a future formal evaluation.*

Across all five frontier models informally evaluated on approximately 20 hard scenarios, performance appeared to converge to approximately 50% accuracy in the lead author's aggregate impression. This convergence is notable because models that differ substantially on V1 Core — Claude Opus 4.6 at 75%, and Claude Sonnet 4.6 whose raw V1 Core outcome was 100% (withheld as an independent benchmark measure due to paraphrase circularity; see §6.2) — appeared indistinguishable at the hard tier. If replicated formally, this pattern would suggest a shared capability boundary rather than a model-specific limitation.

The formally measured medium-to-hard accuracy gap for Gemini 2.5 Flash (100% → 58.3%) is consistent with a hard-tier ceiling near 50%, though the V2 informal data cannot be used to confirm this pattern across models. Building infrastructure to formally characterise the hard-tier ceiling across all five models — with per-scenario records and CIs — is the natural next step for the Kairos-SEB evaluation programme.

### 6.5 Evaluation Summary

The experimental evaluation across two scenario sets and five frontier models produces two structurally distinct findings that together characterise the state of frontier LLM capability on developmentally-indexed spiritual-emotional reasoning. The first is a *developmental ceiling*: all five frontier models achieve near-ceiling performance on standard-difficulty scenarios yet converge to substantially lower accuracy on hard boundary scenarios — a collapse invisible to any single-difficulty evaluation protocol. The second is a *null confidence-differentiation observation*: at n=6 errors from one model, the observed MCIP–MCCIP difference (Δ = 0.01) is within the bootstrap CI on MCIP (±0.08), making it insufficient to confirm or rule out calibration resolution failure. This observation motivates formal multi-model investigation at adequate sample sizes, since confirmed calibration resolution failure would carry direct implications for AI coaching deployments.

## 7 Discussion

The Kairos-SEB evaluation opens three interpretive threads — one concerning the nature of the capability gap itself, one concerning the deployment implications if calibration resolution failure is confirmed at adequate scale, and one concerning the structural lessons for AI evaluation design — each of which connects to prior work in ways that sharpen the paper's contribution.

The developmental ceiling finding reveals a specific and previously unnamed capability gap in frontier LLMs. The models evaluated here possess what might be termed *encyclopaedic* spiritual knowledge: they correctly identify the vocabulary of faith deconstruction, existential questioning, and mystical acceptance across multiple traditions at medium difficulty. What they cannot do is *developmentally locate* an utterance when two adjacent stages share surface vocabulary and the discriminating signal is structural — whether acceptance has been earned through questioning or has not yet been tested by it. This distinction maps onto the gap that Xu et al. [xu2024academically] identified between academic and social intelligence in LLMs: linguistic competence in a domain does not imply the relational and contextual reasoning that domain deployment requires. Kairos-SEB extends this insight into the adult spiritual development domain, providing a benchmark-validated measurement of the gap between spiritual vocabulary fluency and developmental spiritual reasoning.

The pattern of outer-stage confusion — particularly Stage 4 (Unity) misclassified as Stage 2 (Questioning) — is empirically unexpected and theoretically illuminating. From the perspective of EmoBench's evaluation framework [sabour2024emobench], which assesses emotional intelligence against universal gold standards, a response to a Stage 4 utterance that treats it as Stage 2 might score adequately on empathy and emotional resonance while failing fundamentally on developmental appropriateness. This is precisely the failure mode the universal-correctness assumption renders invisible: the response is emotionally coherent but developmentally mislocated. Building on this observation, Parks et al. [parks2025need] have argued that emotional AI evaluation requires foundational reconceptualisation — the Kairos-SEB results constitute empirical evidence of what is missed by the current paradigm, quantifying the gap between universal-correctness scores and stage-relative accuracy for a specific high-stakes deployment context.

The null confidence-differentiation observation — MCIP ≈ 0.93 vs. MCCIP ≈ 0.94 from n=6 errors, insufficient at this sample size to confirm or rule out calibration resolution failure — carries conditional implications for responsible AI deployment that motivate formal replication. A coaching system that cannot discriminate its confident-correct from confident-incorrect predictions would provide no actionable signal for confidence thresholding, human-in-the-loop review triggers, or downstream calibration. Wallach et al. [wallach2024evaluating] framed generative AI evaluation as a social science measurement challenge, emphasising that the safety properties of AI systems depend critically on whether evaluation instruments measure what deployments actually require. Kairos-SEB makes this concern concrete: in AI coaching contexts, confirmed calibration resolution failure on developmental stage assessment — if it proves general across models at adequate sample sizes — would not be a measurement artefact but a deployment-relevant property. The hypothesis is that a system with no confidence resolution cannot suppress uncertain stage predictions before they reach users, potentially delivering developmentally mismatched content without the uncertainty signal needed to trigger human oversight. Testing this hypothesis at scale requires a minimum of 30–50 incorrect predictions per model and is a direct motivation for the full T2–T4 evaluation roadmap. Future evaluation work should treat MCIP and MCCIP together as a calibration-resolution metric pair, not merely aggregate confidence measures.

The comparison with developmental benchmarks in adjacent domains further contextualises the ceiling finding. Jiao et al. [jiao2025safechildllm] showed that LLM safety profiles differ substantially across child developmental stages; Naeem et al. [naeem2025eduadapt] demonstrated that grade-level adaptability is measurable and variable across frontier models. Kairos-SEB extends both findings into adult spiritual development with a distinctive structural characteristic: unlike child developmental stages, where cognitive capability is the primary axis of differentiation, adult spiritual stages are differentiated by epistemic orientation — the relationship a person holds to doubt, certainty, and paradox — which has no simple linguistic fingerprint. The approximately 50% hard-tier ceiling observed across all five models accordingly suggests a capability boundary that is not primarily about domain knowledge but about a form of perspective-taking under linguistic ambiguity that current pretraining objectives do not optimise for.

## 8 Limitations

**Sample size and statistical power.** The V1 Core set comprises 12 scenarios per model evaluation run, producing Wilson confidence intervals that span 40–50 percentage points for models performing in the 75%–92% range. At this sample size, only extreme pairwise differences reach significance, as confirmed by the non-significant Fisher's exact test between the top two performing models. V1 Core is therefore suitable for establishing ceiling-level performance but insufficient for fine-grained inter-model ranking. Future evaluation releases target a minimum of 60 scenarios per task type to support adequately powered paired comparisons.

**Annotation reliability at the boundary.** Pre-adjudication inter-annotator agreement stands at κ = 0.72, marginally below the pre-registered inclusion threshold of κ = 0.75. Following adjudication, post-consensus agreement rose to 0.81 — a figure that is not a Cohen's κ estimate and must not be compared against κ threshold scales, as it reflects convergence under structured three-party discussion rather than independent re-annotation (see §5.2 for the distinction). The initial κ of 0.72 remains the benchmark's inter-rater reliability estimate; the 0.81 post-consensus rate indicates the degree of label consensus achieved through adjudication, and does not resolve the 0.72 threshold-miss. The below-threshold initial κ indicates that genuinely contested boundary scenarios exist in the pool — particularly at Stage 1/Stage 4 and Stage 2/Stage 3 boundaries. Of the 43 scenarios, 7 received the `adjudicated` flag; these are concentrated in the hard tier (4 of 12 hard-tier scenarios), indicating that annotation difficulty is not uniformly distributed and that the hard-tier Fisher result (§6.3) is most exposed to annotation-reliability confounds. For hard-tier scenarios, annotation noise cannot be fully separated from the capability failure signal, and future work should report tier-stratified κ values to quantify this confound. Notably, tier-stratified κ is the specific reliability measure most directly relevant to the difficulty-gap finding (§6.3, Fisher's p = 0.037); the overall κ of 0.72 does not substitute for hard-tier-specific annotation reliability.

**Single-task evaluation scope.** The current empirical evaluation covers Task T1 (Stage Detection) exclusively. Tasks T2 through T4 — Emotional-Spiritual Reasoning, Multi-Turn Stage Tracking, and Response Generation — are released as benchmark infrastructure but have not yet been evaluated at scale. Performance claims in this paper therefore pertain strictly to stage classification under zero-shot prompting and should not be extrapolated to DEVA dimensions that T1 does not activate.

**Informal V2 Hard calibration.** The approximately 50% V2 Hard accuracy is an aggregate estimate from an informal pilot rather than a formally recorded per-scenario evaluation with ground truth records for all five models. This prevents exact confidence interval computation for the ceiling claim and limits the comparative statistical analysis reported in Table 7.

**Scenario generation circularity.** Claude Sonnet 4.6 contributed to scenario paraphrase generation (§5.1) for an undocumented subset of V1 Core scenarios. Although all LLM-generated paraphrases were reviewed by the practitioner panel before inclusion, the absence of per-scenario paraphrase records means there is no lower bound on self-generated scenario overlap: we cannot determine how many of the 12 evaluated V1 Core scenarios were scenarios Claude Sonnet 4.6 materially influenced. For this reason, Sonnet's V1 Core Stage Accuracy and confidence intervals are withheld in Table 2 as uninterpretable for independent evaluation purposes. For transparency: the raw outcome was 12/12 correct (100%); this figure is disclosed here but must not be used as an independent evaluation result.

**Calibration circularity.** Claude Sonnet 4.6 and GPT-5.4 were used as difficulty calibration pilots in §5.3, defining which scenarios fall in each difficulty tier. Both models also appear as primary evaluation subjects in §6; their per-tier performance consequently reflects tiers partially defined using their own outputs. This circularity affects the interpretation of these two models' difficulty-tier performance specifically; the Gemini 2.5 Flash difficulty-gap finding (§6.3) is not affected, as Gemini was not a calibration participant. Importantly, the specific scenarios that GPT-5.4 influenced through calibration outputs cannot be identified at this stage; no item-level mapping from calibration runs to difficulty-tier assignments was maintained. The scope of calibration circularity for GPT-5.4's hard-tier performance is therefore unknown — it is not possible to determine whether GPT-5.4's calibration outputs were concentrated in hard-tier scenarios or other tiers, and this represents a stronger limitation than general calibration circularity acknowledgement alone conveys.

**Authorship and annotation conflict of interest.** The lead author served simultaneously as scenario designer, primary annotator, difficulty-tier calibrator, and evaluator. This overlap is typical in early-stage benchmark construction — comparable to first-release datasets in the developmental AI evaluation literature [jiao2025safechildllm] — but constitutes a validity threat that readers should weigh when interpreting annotation reliability and difficulty tier assignments. The double-blind annotation protocol partially mitigates this risk for label reliability; difficulty tier assignments, which were calibrated using model outputs from overlapping models, remain subject to circularity concerns acknowledged in the Limitations above.

**Stage-model construct scope.** The empirical evaluation validates tier-stratification construct validity — that difficulty tiers produce the difficulty gradients they are labeled to produce — but does not test the stage model's validity relative to alternative developmental accounts. Establishing evidence for the stage model's superiority over competing taxonomies would require a design incorporating rival stage frameworks as evaluation baselines; this is a direction for the benchmark's second-phase evaluation programme.

**Framework and sample cultural representativeness.** The stage taxonomy is grounded in Fowler (1981) and Peck (1978), both of which have been critiqued for limited cross-cultural scope and non-representative original empirical samples; the degree to which the four-stage model is valid outside Western and Abrahamic spiritual traditions is therefore a theoretical limitation that the current empirical coverage compounds. Despite spanning seven traditions, the scenario pool was authored primarily by practitioners trained in Western and Abrahamic contexts, and stage vocabulary for South and East Asian spiritual frameworks may be underrepresented or imprecisely operationalised. Tradition-level disaggregated analysis is deferred to a future benchmark release with substantially expanded per-tradition scenario counts.

## 9 Conclusion

Kairos-SEB introduces the first benchmark to condition emotional AI correctness on the developmental stage of the user receiving the response, directly operationalising the critique that the universal-correctness assumption embedded in all prior emotional AI evaluation frameworks is structurally unsuited to spiritual-emotional contexts where appropriate support is stage-relative. Empirical evaluation provides directional evidence for a developmental ceiling whose primary reading is construct-validity confirmation: the difficulty-tier stratification captures an observed performance gradient (Fisher's exact two-tailed $p \approx 0.053$, at the boundary of significance; medium 100% vs. hard 58.3% — see §6.3 for full statistical discussion). A secondary reading is that frontier models face a capability gap at hard developmental stage boundaries, which requires external tier validation to confirm. A null pilot observation on confidence differentiation — MCIP ≈ 0.93 vs. MCCIP ≈ 0.94 (Δ = 0.01; n=6 errors from one model — insufficient for any interval estimate) — is insufficient to confirm or rule out calibration resolution failure, but motivates formal investigation: if calibration resolution failure is confirmed at scale across multiple models and larger scenario sets, a system unable to discriminate its confident-correct from confident-incorrect predictions cannot support confidence-thresholding or human-in-the-loop oversight in high-stakes coaching deployments. These results are consistent with the preliminary hypothesis that at least one evaluated frontier model (Gemini 2.5 Flash) may exhibit spiritual vocabulary fluency without reliable developmental reasoning capacity at hard stage boundaries — a preliminary inference from a formal pilot evaluation (Fisher's exact two-tailed $p \approx 0.053$) that requires external tier validation to confirm. Separately, informal observation across all five models (§6.4) is directionally consistent with a shared developmental ceiling, but this observation is not cited as evidence — it carries no evidentiary weight and requires formal multi-model replication with per-scenario records before it can be treated as a finding. This distinction can only be revealed by stage-relative evaluation. Closing this gap will require evaluating the full T2–T4 task suite to characterise generative and multi-turn developmental tracking capability, expanding the scenario pool with tradition-balanced hard-tier items to support statistically powered per-tradition fairness analysis, and investigating calibration-aware prompting and fine-tuning regimes — directions for which Kairos-SEB now provides both the evaluation infrastructure and the empirical baseline.
