## Abstract

The neurobiological disease model of addiction has produced important advances but systematically fails to account for three empirical anomalies: high rates of natural recovery without treatment, the graded dose-response relationship between relational trauma and addiction risk, and the dramatic effect of social environment on substance self-administration in animal models.

This review synthesises convergent evidence across five independent domains to argue that addiction is fundamentally a disorder of connection, emerging as an adaptive response to chronic relational deprivation.

**Five evidence streams:**
1. Neuroscience — drugs of abuse activate the same mesolimbic circuits evolved for social bonding
2. Trauma / ACEs — childhood relational trauma predicts addiction severity in a dose-response relationship
3. Social capital — community disconnection predicts overdose mortality at population level
4. Environmental / naturalistic — Rat Park, Vietnam veteran data, Portugal decriminalisation
5. Treatment outcomes — therapeutic alliance, AA's social network mechanism, peer support

**The Relational Deprivation Model:** recovery is not the removal of the solution but the provision of genuine belonging.
